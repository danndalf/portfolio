How does the internet work?

The Internet, from its foundation to today, is built from a network of networks. When research started into the underpinnings of the technology that would eventually fuel the modern Internet, the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) was focused on exchanging data between multiple computers in a way that could withstand signal loss in some of its connections. From its original conception, the Internet has been decentralized—no single entity controlled the entire network.


A Section of the Routes of the Internet

This network of networks started off as ARPANET in 1969. MILNET, for use by the United States military, separated from ARPANET in 1983, and the growing inter-network and was later joined by the National Science Foundation-backed NSFNet in 1985. Since those days, the architecture of the Internet hasn't fundamentally changed. It's still a network of interconnected networks.

Multiple protocols, not just WWW

  1. Internet is not the World Wide Web
  2. Internet is a global network of computer networks
  3. WWW is a system of hyperlinked documents (web pages) accessed via the internet.
  4. TCP/IP is the primary networking protocol used by the internet
  5. Higher level protocols sit on top of the internet (HTTP, IRC)
  1. darknets (tor, i2p, freenet) are internets hidden within the internet

  1. Image: Chaos Computer Club logo

  1. Image: Department of Defense logo
  2. March: the US Department of Defense declared TCP/IP as the standard for all military computer networking.
  3. DNS Invented to replace manual distribution of HOSTS.TXT files

1983

  1. Image: Map of one of the first TCP/IP networks
  2. TCP/IP replaces NCP as the protocol of ARPANET, focused on resource sharing
  3. MILNET Splits off of ARPANET, giving the US Military its own network
  4. TCSEC, the “NSA Rainbow book series”, released

1984

  1. Image: The Cover of 2600
  2. 2600 starts publication
  3. Computer Fraud and Abuse Act passes, replacing the wiretapping law previously used to prosecute hackers and phreakers

1989

  2. First Commercial ISP founded (PSINet)

1990

  1. Image: EFF Logo
  2. Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF), one of the most prolific digital rights defense groups, is founded by John Gilmore, John Perry Barlow and Mitch Kapor

Barlow described the visit as "complicated by [The Agent's] fairly complete unfamiliarity with computer technology. 1991

  1. Image: IDEA Cipher block diagram

1992

  3. The movie Sneakers is released.

1993

  1. Image: DEF CON “Human” badge
  2. Zimmermann became the formal target of a criminal investigation by the US Government for "munitions export without a license". Cryptosystems using keys larger than 40 bits were then considered munitions within the definition of the US export regulations; PGP has never used keys smaller than 128 bits so it qualified at that time. Penalties for violation, if found guilty, were substantial. After several years, the investigation of Zimmermann was closed without filing criminal charges against him or anyone else.
  3. June: First DEFCON conference is held, an annual congregation of hackers from around the world in Nevada.

1995

  1. Image: FREE KEVIN sticker

  3. The movie Hackers is released.
  4. Commercial traffic first allowed on the Internet

1998

  2. L0pht Heavy Industries testifies in congress about computer security weaknesses within the US government
  3. DMCA goes into effect.

1999

  1. Image: Napster Logo

  3. That Napster was responsible for vicarious infringement of the plaintiffs' copyrights.
